Senate Reform: Why the Senate Doesn't Represent the US

Photo Credit: Martin Falbisoner

Indroduction

The United States Senate is the upper chamber of the United States Congress, which along with the House of Representatives constitutes the legislature of the United States. The Senate is currently composed of 100 senators, two for each state. They serve staggered terms of six years.

The Senate has unique advice and consent power to ratify treaties, declare war (theoretically), and confirm Cabinet Secretaries, Supreme Court Justices, federal judges, ambassadors, and other officials. It also conducts impeachment trials and, of course, makes law. It is designed to protect the power of individual states, and particularly smaller states with less representation in the House of Representatives.

Given the amount of power and influence that the Senate wields, it is important to ensure that it appropriately represents the American people. This page is intended to show some ways in which it is falling short of that ideal.

Senate and Population Data Analysis

The Senate was not created to be a body representative of the US population, but rather of the states. This was a logical step in the time of the Founding Fathers, when each state was more like unto its own country than a part of a nation. Back then, the US was a little more like the European Union is today: largely self-governed except for some situations in which acting in concert is valuable. Now, however, this is no longer the case. You probably think of yourself as an American far more readily than a Pennsylvanian or a Texan. And even as the states grow more similar and united, the range of their populations has grown more and more extreme, leading to what we see today.

18 Senators represent 50% of the population. More than half of the US states could want something to occur, and they might not even be halfway to a majority.



This also leads to skews along partisan lines: because larger and more populous states tend to be Democratic, the Democrats represent 40 million more people than Republicans despite exactly equal numbers in the Senate.
